Detailed Description

template<int dim>
class Forest::EqTransport< dim >

Neutron transport equation.

This class implements the transport equation at two different levels. We have to solve a source problem in a multi-group level, and this is approximated by working with single-group solvers.

Multi-Group (Gauss-Seidel)

We have to solve the following source problem

\begin{align*} L \Psi = MSD \Psi + Q \end{align*}

Where the source \( Q \) may contain the fission source scaled with the multiplication factor and other external sources. This system has a block structure defined by the energy discretization, where the streaming operator \( L \) is block diagonal (no coupling between different groups), and operator \( S \) is "almost" lower triangular (up-scattering is usually smaller than down-scattering). A reasonable approach would be then to solve the systems as if it would be lower triangular, and "lagger" the up-scattering to be multiplied to a guess. To do so, we start solving the system for the first energy group, and then applying the Gauss-Seidel method as follows

\begin{align*} (L_g+MS_{g \to g}D)\Psi_{g}^{n+1} = \sum_{g'=1}^{g-1}M S_{g' \to g} D \Psi_{g'}^{n+1} + \sum_{g'=g+1}^{G}M S_{g' \to g} D \Psi_{g'}^{n} + Q_{g} \quad g = 1, \ldots, G . \end{align*}

In order to simplify the notation, grouping right hand side in a unique source per group

\begin{align*} & \hat{Q}_{g} = \sum_{g'=1}^{g-1}M S_{g' \to g} D \Psi_{g'}^{n+1} + \sum_{g'=g+1}^{G}M S_{g' \to g} D \Psi_{g'}^{n} + Q_{g} \quad g = 1, \ldots, G . \end{align*}

we rewrite the system as

\begin{align*} & (L_g+MS_{g \to g}D)\Psi_{g}^{n+1} = \hat{Q}_{g} \quad g = 1, \ldots, G \end{align*}

Note
It is important to notice here that using this strategy we are not really solving the system. In order to do so, this iteration can be used as a preconditioner for a full energy groups GMRES. The update obtained here is a good approximation, and it is enough when using the power iteration to solve the eigenvalue problem, while it would not be enough if the eigenproblem is solved with, for instance, an Arnoldi method.

Single Group Source Iteration.

Here we perform, for a given energy group m_g, one source iteration. Lets consider the following problem (we skip the group index \( g \) because it happens inside each group)

\begin{align*} L \Psi = MSD \Psi + Q \end{align*}

Of course we can directly try to invert the matrix \( (L-MSD)\) and get the solution \( \Psi = (L-MSD)^{-1} Q\), but this system is very large, and its structure suggests us a more efficient procedure, called source iteration.

Now, the streaming operator can be decomposed into the part without reflecting boundary conditions, \( L_{0} \) , and the coupling between the different directions due to the reflecting boundary conditions, \( L_{R} \) , as follows

\begin{align*} L = L_{0} + L_{R} \end{align*}

Then, two scenarios are considered. The first one neglect reflective boundary conditions, in order to simplify the explanation, and the second one includes reflecting boundary conditions.

Neglecting Reflecting Boundary Conditions

Here we assume \( L_{R} \equiv 0 \), so \( L = L_{0} \) is a block diagonal matrix that can be easily inverted by performing transport sweeps. We "lagged" the scattering term to a previous iteration step, we use the flux in the previous iteration collapsed with the operator D to reduce its dimensionality, and then update the solution inverting the streaming term (what should be easier)

\begin{align*} & \Psi^{l+1} = L^{-1}(MS \Phi^{l} + Q) \\ & \Phi^{l} = D \Psi^{l} \end{align*}

A useful trick here, is to use, instead of the source \( Q \), what is called the uncollided source \( b = L^{-1} Q \), and then perform the following iteration procedure

\begin{align*} & \Psi^{l+1} = L^{-1} MS \Phi^{l} + b \\ & \Phi^{l} = D \Psi^{l} \end{align*}

This expression, ignoring boundary conditions, can be simplified further by combining the two equations as follows

\begin{align*} & \Phi^{l+1} = D L^{-1} MS \Phi^{l} + D b \end{align*}

what is equivalent to solve the system

\begin{align*} & (I - D L^{-1} MS) \Phi = D b \end{align*}

Including Reflecting Boundary Conditions

Here we assume "lagged" the scattering term to a previous iteration step, as before, but also "lagged" the effect of the boundary conditions, and we use the flux in the previous iteration collapsed with the operator D to reduce its dimensionality. Thus we update the solution inverting the streaming term (block diagonal)

\begin{align*} & \Psi^{l+1} = L_{0}^{-1}(MS \Phi^{l} - L_{R}\Psi^{l}) + b \\ & \Phi^{l} = D \Psi^{l} \end{align*}

where here \( b \) is defined as the uncollided flux \( b := L_{0}^{-1} Q \), without considering the boundary conditions. Now we can not remove the angular flux combining the two equations because of the boundary conditions. Instead, we are going to consider the boundary conditions applying to the vector of significant dofs, that is projected to the full vector through the operator \( P \) , and we consider our state vector the vector of the scalar flux and the vector of significant angular dofs, as follows

\begin{align*} & \Psi^{l+1} = L_{0}^{-1}(MS \Phi^{l} - L_{R}P\Psi_{S}^{l}) + b \\ & \Phi^{l} = D \Psi^{l} \\ & \Psi_{S}^{l+1} = P^{T}\Psi^{l+1} \end{align*}

that can be reduced to

\begin{align*} & \Phi^{l+1} = D L_{0}^{-1}(MS \Phi^{l} - L_{R}P\Psi_{S}^{l}) + D b \\ & \Psi_{S}^{l+1} = P^{T}L_{0}^{-1}(MS \Phi^{l} - L_{R}P\Psi_{S}^{l}) + P^{T}b \end{align*}

Or in a matrix form, as follows

\begin{align*} \left[ \begin{array}{c} \Phi^{l+1} \\ \Psi^{l+1}_{S} \end{array} \right] = \left[ \begin{array}{cc} D & 0 \\ 0 & I \end{array} \right] \left( \left[ \begin{array}{c} I \\ P^{T} \end{array} \right] L_{0}^{-1} \left[ \begin{array}{cc} I & -L_{R} P \end{array} \right] \right) \left[ \begin{array}{cc} M & 0 \\ 0 & I \end{array} \right] \left[ \begin{array}{cc} S & 0 \\ 0 & I \end{array} \right] \left[ \begin{array}{c} \Phi^{l} \\ \Psi^{l}_{S} \end{array} \right] + \left[ \begin{array}{c} D b \\ P^{T}b \end{array} \right] \end{align*}

This can be rewritten as

\begin{align*} & \hat{\Phi}^{l+1} = \hat{D} \hat{L}^{-1} \hat{M}\hat{S} \hat{\Phi}^{l} + \hat{b} \end{align*}

where

\begin{align*} \hat{\Phi} := \left[ \begin{array}{c} \Phi\\ \Psi_{S} \end{array} \right], \quad \hat{L}^{-1} := \left( \left[ \begin{array}{c} I \\ P^{T} \end{array} \right] L_{0}^{-1} \left[ \begin{array}{cc} I & -L_{R} P \end{array} \right] \right) \equiv \left[ \begin{array}{cc} L_{0}^{-1} & -L_{0}^{-1} L_{R} P \\ P^{T}L_{0}^{-1} & -P^{T}L_{0}^{-1} L_{R} P \end{array} \right] \end{align*}

and the rest of the operators follow the notation in the obvious way.

Attention
In the paper [1] the vector \( \hat{b} \) has the second component equal to zero, while here I obtain that the second component is equal to \( P^{T} b \). Check if it should be the case.

We can also rearrange this system to use a Krylov solver as follows

\begin{align*} & (\hat{I} - \hat{D} \hat{L}^{-1} \hat{M}\hat{S}) \hat{\Phi} = \hat{b} \end{align*}

Application of matrix to vector src, and write result into dst.

Here we perform, for a given energy group m_g, one source iteration. In a matrix form it can be expressed as follows

\begin{align*} & \hat{x} = \hat{D} \hat{L}^{-1} \hat{M}\hat{S} \hat{b} \end{align*}

We use the following notation for the arguments:

\begin{align*} \text{src} := b \equiv \left[ \begin{array}{c} b_{0} \\ b_{S} \end{array} \right] \in \mathbb{R}^{I \times M} \times \mathbb{R}^{S} , \quad \text{and} \quad \text{dst} := x \equiv \left[ \begin{array}{c} x_{0} \\ x_{S} \end{array} \right] \in \mathbb{R}^{I \times M} \times \mathbb{R}^{S} \end{align*}

Where \( I \) is the number of degrees of freedom for the spatial discretization over the mesh, \( M \) is the number of moments in the scattering operator, and \( S \) is the number of significant degrees of freedom for the boundary conditions.

We perform the following steps:

  • The first thing is to initialize the vector \( x \) to zero

    \begin{align*} x := \left[ \begin{array}{c} x_{0} \\ x_{S} \end{array} \right] = \left[ \begin{array}{c} 0 \\ 0 \end{array} \right] \end{align*}

  • Then we put the scattering generated into m_rhs

    \begin{align*} v_{1} = S b_{0} \end{align*}

  • Now, for every direction n we do the following operations

    \begin{align*} & \text{for n = 1:N} & \\ &&& v_{2} = M_{n} v_{1} \\ &&& v_{2} = v_{2} - P L_{R,n} b_{S,n} \\ &&& v_{3} = L_0^{-1} v_{2} \\ &&& x_{S} = x_{S} + P^{T} v_{3} \\ &&& x_{0} = x_{0} + D_{n} v_{3} \\ & \text{endfor} & \end{align*}

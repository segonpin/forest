template<int dim>
class Forest::HomData< dim >

HomData.

The homogenization in space and condensation in energy is performed by using the following definitions

Homogenization and condensation of transport cross sections

To understand the process of homogenization in space and condensation in energy we can show the following two meshes

*            r1 r2 r3 r4
*
*            R1   R2  R3
*           _____________
* g1   G1   |  |  :  |  |
*           _____________
* g2        |  |  :  |  |
*      G2   ----..-..----
* g3        |  |  :  |  |
*           _____________
* g4   G3   |  |  :  |  |
*           _____________
*

where {r1, r2, r3, r4} are the regions composing the fine mesh for the space, and {g1, g2, g3, g4} is the fine mesh for the energy. The coarsening is done by defining coarse regions {R1, R2, R3} and {G1, G2, G3} in such a way that the coarse mesh match the fine mesh, where the coarse regions contains at least one fine region (for example R1 = {r1}), but possibly more (for example G2 = {g2, g3} ).

Then we define the flux in a region \( r\) as the absolute value of the flux for this region (in space and energy)

\begin{align*} \Psi_{r,g} = \int_{V_{r}} \Psi_{g}(\mathbf{x}) \ \text{d}\mathbf{x} \end{align*}

and the flux in the macro region (in space and energy)

\begin{align*} \Psi_{\mathbf{r},\mathbf{g}} = \sum_{g \in \mathbf{g}} \sum_{r \in \mathbf{r}} \Psi_{r,g} \end{align*}

Thus we use these quantities to perform a flux weighting of the cross sections in the different regions (in space and energy) as follows

\begin{align*} \Sigma^{T}_{\mathbf{r},\mathbf{g}} & = \frac{\sum_{g \in \mathbf{g}} \sum_{r \in \mathbf{r}} \Sigma^{T}_{r,g} \Psi_{r,g} } {\Psi_{\mathbf{r},\mathbf{g}} } \end{align*}

\begin{align*} \Sigma^{F}_{\mathbf{r},\mathbf{g}} & = \frac{\sum_{g \in \mathbf{g}} \sum_{r \in \mathbf{r}} \Sigma^{F}_{r,g} \Psi_{r,g} }{\Psi_{\mathbf{r},\mathbf{g}} } \end{align*}

\begin{align*} \nu \Sigma^{F}_{\mathbf{r},\mathbf{g}} & = \frac{\sum_{g \in \mathbf{g}} \sum_{r \in \mathbf{r}} \nu \Sigma^{F}_{r,g} \Psi_{r,g} }{\Psi_{\mathbf{r},\mathbf{g}} } \end{align*}

\begin{align*} \Sigma^{S}_{\mathbf{r},\mathbf{g} \to \mathbf{g}'} & = \frac{\sum_{g \in \mathbf{g}} \sum_{g ' \in \mathbf{g} '} \sum_{r \in \mathbf{r}} \Sigma^S_{r,g \to g '} \Psi_{r,g} } {\Psi_{\mathbf{r},\mathbf{g}} } \end{align*}

\begin{align*} \chi_{\mathbf{r},\mathbf{g}} & = \frac{\sum_{r \in \mathbf{r}} \sum_{g \in \mathbf{g}} \chi_{r,g} \sum_{g ' = 1}^G \nu \Sigma^F_{r,g '} \Psi_{r,g '} } {\sum_{r \in\mathbf{r}} \sum_{g '' = 1}^G \chi_{r,g ''} \sum_{g ' = 1}^G \nu \Sigma^{F}_{r,g '} \Psi_{r,g '} } = \frac{ \sum_{r \in \mathbf{r}} f_{r} \sum_{g \in \mathbf{g}} \chi_{r,g} } { \sum_{r \in\mathbf{r}} f_{r} \underbrace{ \sum_{g '' = 1}^G \chi_{r,g ''} }_{=1} } \\ & = \sum_{r \in \mathbf{r}} \frac{ f_{r} } { \sum_{r \in\mathbf{r}} f_{r} } \sum_{g \in \mathbf{g}} \chi_{r,g} = \sum_{r \in \mathbf{r}} \frac{f_{r}}{\sum_{r \in\mathbf{r}} f_{r} } \chi_{r,\mathbf{g}} \end{align*}

\begin{align*} \Sigma^{tr}_{\mathbf{r},g} & = \frac{\sum_{r \in \mathbf{r}} \Sigma^{tr}_{r,g} \Psi_{r,g} } {\sum_{r \in \mathbf{r}} \Psi_{r,g} } \end{align*}

Homogenization and condensation of diffusion coefficient

The diffusion coefficient is usually homogenized, which is defined as one third of the inverse of the transport cross section, should be considered a bit in detail.

It is calculated by first homogenizing in space the transport cross section,

\begin{align*} D_{\mathbf{r},g} = \frac{1}{3 \Sigma^{tr}_{\mathbf{r},g}} \end{align*}

and then the standard formula for the energy condensation

\begin{align*} D_{\mathbf{r},\mathbf{g}} = \frac{\sum_{g \in \mathbf{g}} D_{\mathbf{r},g} \Psi_{\mathbf{r},g}}{\Psi_{\mathbf{r},\mathbf{g}}} = \frac{\sum_{g \in \mathbf{g}} \frac{1}{3 \Sigma^{tr}_{\mathbf{r},g}} \Psi_{\mathbf{r},g}}{\Psi_{\mathbf{r},\mathbf{g}}} \end{align*}

Absorbtion cross section (to be used with the diffusion equation)

The absorbtion cross section \( \Sigma^A_{\mathbf{g}} \) is defined as

\begin{align*} \Sigma^A_{{g}} \equiv \Sigma^T_{g} - \sum_{\substack{g' = 1}}^{G} \Sigma^S_{g \to g'} \end{align*}

Removal cross section (to be used with the diffusion equation)

The removal cross section \( \Sigma^R_{\mathbf{g}} \) is defined as

\begin{align*} \Sigma^R_{{g}} \equiv \Sigma^A_{g} + \sum_{\substack{g' = 1 \\ g' \neq g}}^{G} \Sigma^S_{g \to g'} \end{align*}

or alternatively, using the definition of the absortion cross section, as

\begin{align*} \Sigma^R_{{g}} & \equiv \Sigma^A_{g} + \sum_{\substack{g' = 1 \\ g' \neq g}}^{G} \Sigma^S_{g \to g'} \\ & = \Sigma^T_{g} - \sum_{\substack{g' = 1}}^{G} \Sigma^S_{g \to g'} + \sum_{\substack{g' = 1 \\ g' \neq g}}^{G} \Sigma^S_{g \to g'} \\ & = \Sigma^T_{g} - \Sigma^S_{g \to g} \end{align*}

If axial buckling is specified the removal cross section writes as

\begin{align*} \Sigma^R_{g} \equiv D_{g} B_z^2 + \Sigma^A_{g} + \sum_{\substack{g' = 1 \\ g' \neq g}}^{G} \Sigma^S_{g \to g'} \end{align*}
